AI: Those are fascinating research challenges! Can you describe a specific recent project where you used AI for research like this? What role did it play, and how did that feel for you as a writer?

User: The most recent was the sparking-weapons one. Then I asked for more information about the history of steelmaking to gauge whether the kind of high-carbon steel necessary to create sparks would be likely to exist commonly in that world, given where it is technologically (though I didn't provide any details about that). AI: How did that interaction feel for you as you were working through those questions? When you got those answers about the sparks and steel, how did that influence what you wrote?

User: It didn't really influence what I wrote. AI: What concerns, if any, do you have about AI's presence in your field of writing?

User: I'm not against the possibility of people who aren't writers, artists, etc. using AI tools to create art they wouldn't be able to otherwise. My only concern is the risk of blurring the line between purely human-created content and content that's been heavily influenced, edited, refined or even largely created by AI.

AI: Tell me more about that concern with blurring the line. What would that look like in practice, and why does that worry you?

User: Compare it to a hand-carved, hand-painted sculpture versus a premade, hand-painted sculpture versus a 3D-printed, full-colour sculpture from a pattern. The third might look just as good, but it doesn't require any skill,  just picking a pattern and hitting a button. The second is more impressive and requires time, effort and skill, and the first exhibits the most skill and effort and is the most impressive. But that's fine, because you can easily tell the difference between the three. It can be a lot harder to tell with text or even image-based art, and it'll become harder and harder to tell as AI becomes more advanced.
