However, other days, I use AI specifically to generate project briefs that will be used by myself or other writers to create content for prominent clients.

AI: That's interesting - you're using AI in quite different ways depending on the situation. 

Tell me more about those project briefs you mentioned. When you're creating briefs for yourself or other writers, what does that process typically look like?

User: I'll typically have a template based on specific client guidelines. For example, the template will include things like tone (professional, casual, friendly, etc.), word counts, required section headers, links to include, whether or not the client requires the Oxford comma, etc. I'll feed that template into the model and then give it the subject for this particular article. For example, let's say the client is a genealogy site and my company is providing value-add articles. If we're doing articles on different holidays I might have a template for a specific holiday and then tell the AI to create a new brief in that format for a different holiday.

AI: So you're essentially using the AI to replicate a format you've already established but adapt it for new content topics. 

When you hand those AI-generated briefs to other writers, how do they typically respond to working from something that was created by AI? Do they know it came from AI, and does that affect how they use it?

User: For me, there's usually a middle man - a project manager that's assigned me these templates to  create as paying work. As long as they provide all the information the writer know, they probably don't really care.

AI: That makes sense - if the brief has all the necessary information, the origin probably doesn't matter much to the writer doing the work.

Plus, I'm a high-level writer at my company, so there are certain expectations that I wouldn't trust the chatbot to fulfill. I'm fine with giving AI supporting tasks, but I still want to be the star of the show when it comes to the actual written content.

AI: So you're using AI almost like a research assistant to point you in the right direction, then doing the verification work yourself.

It had been fine for most of the articles, but then every once in a while I'd have these problems and would have to keep trying again to finesse the articles. I was getting paid per brief, so that really affected how much I was getting paid per hour.

AI: That sounds frustrating - especially when it's affecting your pay rate. The inconsistency must make it hard to predict how much time a task will actually take.

Some of it came back for higher-level copywriters like me, but my company ended up letting go of most of the lower-level copywriters.  I think, though, that it makes me work harder to keep up my skillset so I can compete.
