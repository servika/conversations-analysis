User: I was working on a research project that involved incorporating a heterogeneous catalyst into the recycling and reprocessing of PET plastics. The project was a handover from a previous project that looked at the issues of PET degradation, and how repeated cycles using the same PET caused damage to the polymer framework. The final conclusion made by the previous project manager was that the carboxylic acid end of the monomer would incorrectly degrade to a vinyl end when the polymer was broken apart. This would then react with any water in the system to form an alcohol end, and thus the monomer was no longer able to react in the same way. The reason for this degradation was simply shear stress and strain from the high pressures and temperatures within the system.

My role was to carry this forward. When I took over the project, there were two issues to tackle. The first was that the degradation of PET resulted in many other products being mixed in to the plastic which were able to diffuse into any liquids stored inside it. One of these chemicals was acetaldehyde, and causes a fruity 'off' taste to any liquids stored inside, with a human detection threshold of 60 ppb. Currently, this is removed using anthaniliamide in a 1:1 chemical reaction, which is 70% effective. The abatement of acetaldehyde was considered the priority goal.
The second goal was to try and improve the quality of the PET by reducing degradation. This would make it effective for many more PET cycles.

At the project start, the equipment used to synthesise PET plastics was new. This means that with an operating lifetime of 15 to 20 years, changing the equipment in any way was not possible, and so I had to find a way to achieve my goal without needing any additional engineering. This made me gravitate towards using a heterogeneous catalysis first, as these are easy to remove from feedstocks. 

When I took over this project, I had a meeting with members from two companies that would work together collaboratively on this. The first meeting was to discuss what possible routes that could be taken to succeed with our objectives. We discussed the machinery and the equipment that we could work with - how it works, the conditions inside, limitations - and we came up with a list of things that could be altered during the process. 

The next stage was the information research stage, which I performed independently. This was looking up any information to see if something like this had been done before, and starting to build an information bank to identify any patterns between materials.

Next was the testing phase. A model reactor was built to test the interactions of acetaldehyde over any potential catalysts that had been identified. A gas-phase reactor with a fixed bed was chosen, with a built-in GC-MS to monitor the gas feed to determine how active the catalyst was, how much acetaldehyde was removed, and what products were formed. 1000 ppm of acetaldehyde in argon was chosen as the feed gas mixture to mimic the low concentrations and 'inert' atmospheres (ie total oxidation not possible) of the extruder that is used for reprocessing.
During this phase we tested many different heterogeneous catalysts, from metal oxides and mixed metal oxides, to zeolites, to supported nanoparticles. Once we had an idea of what worked and what didn't, we moved on to the next phase.

The next phase was the incorporation or scale-up phase. Here, we tested our catalysts in the synthesis of PET bottles, which involved making the preform and blowing them to 2 L bottles. We then analysed everything we could about the PET that formed - colour, strength, haze, and of course content of acetaldehyde. The important part here was to determine whether our catalysts actually were able to operate for their intended purposes. Here, only a small number of catalysts proved to be viable, and were nowhere near the 70% benchmark that we had set. Those that showed promise were evaluated and taken back to the testing phase in the model reactor. 

Once catalysts had been pushed to their limits in both the testing and scale-up phases, we evaluated why this might be the case. We looked back to PET plastics and found that some resins were made with antimony metal, while others were made with Nickel. We doped samples of each catalyst with both metals, and re-ran some tests. While those doped with antimony saw the reactivity decrease to almost 0% in every case, those doped with nickel were unaffected. As a final part of the evaluation stage, we tried our catalyst on nickel resins. We found that while the catalyst cleared more acetaldehyde molecules from the PET of nickel resins, it still removed the same percentage of acetaldehyde from both (nickel resins tend to produce up to 50 ppm of acetaldehyde, while antimony resins only produce 10 ppm at maximum). This meant that it was more likely that our catalysts were mobility limited within the reaction stream - this made sense as they were more dense than the PET, so likely sank to the bottom.

The final stage of my involvement in this project was to set some future goals and to summarise what needed to be done for this project to succeed. I concluded that the heterogeneous catalysts we tested had great potential - they clearly removed acetaldehyde from the waste stream, and some of them also catalysed the breakdown of PET using much lower temperatures. I also established why this might be, and set frameworks for the next catalysts that should be produced. I also looked at alternative places where catalysts could be employed and found that one other area is during the blowing of the PET mould, where it is heated to 130 Celsius using IR radiation at approximately 1100nm in wavelength. I found other materials that act as a catalyst only in the presence of IR, and suggested these for the future project leader.

Ultimately, my role in this was to oversee the project from:
Conception > Research > Testing > Scale-up > Evaluation > Conclusions/Future Suggestions
and I would say that I succeeded in this.

I asked it if the two datasets were in complete competition with each other, complete support, or if they weren't quite vibing together but could if we approached our theories from another angle.
